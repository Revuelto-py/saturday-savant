Methodology

Savant Rating SVR

Saturday Savant's signature team strength model: how many points a team's offense and defense are really worth, once you strip away schedule luck, pace, garbage time, and home cooking.

Offensive Rating
Points scored per 10 drives, adjusted for every defense faced. The national average is about 21.5.
Defensive Rating
Points allowed per 10 drives, adjusted for every offense faced. Lower is better.
Net Rating
Offense minus Defense: the margin we'd expect against a perfectly average FBS team on a neutral field.
How It Works
1 · The drive is football's possession

Points per game rewards fast offenses that simply get more chances. SVR measures points per drive instead, then scales by 10 — roughly one game's worth of possessions — so the numbers read like scores. A methodical offense that scores on half its drives rates identically whether it runs 9 possessions a game or 14.

2 · Not every drive counts

Drives that aren't real attempts to score are thrown out before anything is computed: kneel-downs at the end of a half, overtime possessions (which start in field-goal range), and all of garbage time — any drive beginning with the score out of hand for that stage of the game (up 38+ in the 2nd quarter, 28+ in the 3rd, 21+ in the 4th). That's also why SVR needs no artificial cap on blowout wins: a drive maxes out at 8 points, and the padded second half of a 56–0 game simply never enters the sample. Pick-sixes and kick-return touchdowns belong to neither an offense nor the defense that allowed a drive, so they're excluded from both sides. Games against FCS opponents don't count at all.

3 · Every number is opponent-adjusted

Scoring 2.5 points per drive on the best defense in America means something entirely different from doing it against the worst. Each game's efficiency is rescaled by the opponent's own adjusted rating — and since those ratings depend on their opponents too, the whole system is recalculated over and over until every rating is consistent with every other. Home-field advantage (worth about 20% more points per drive in {{ season }}) is measured from the data and removed, so every rating is expressed at a neutral site.

4 · Built to predict, not to reward

Recent games count a little more than September ones, and early in a season every team's small sample is blended toward the national average until it earns its extremes. SVR answers one question: "if these two teams played tonight on a neutral field, what's the margin?" It doesn't care about résumés, rankings, or who deserved to make the playoff — which is exactly why it occasionally disagrees with the polls, and why that disagreement is informative.
